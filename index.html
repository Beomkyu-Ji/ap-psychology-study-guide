Unit 1: Biological Bases of Behavior

The physical systems and processes that underlie psychological phenomena

Unit Overview

The Biological Bases of Behavior unit explores how biological processes influence psychological phenomena. This unit lays the foundation for understanding the physical structures and mechanisms that enable human behavior, thought, and emotion.

Key Topics

  • Interaction of genetics and environment
  • Structure and function of the nervous system
  • Neural communication and neurotransmitters
  • Brain regions and their specialized functions
  • Sleep stages and circadian rhythms
  • Sensation and sensory processing

Why This Unit Matters

Understanding biological bases of behavior provides essential context for all psychological phenomena. Every thought, emotion, and behavior has underlying biological processes. This unit helps you see the connection between physical structures (like neurons and brain regions) and psychological experiences (like memory, emotion, and consciousness).

Exam Significance

Unit 1 represents approximately 15-25% of the AP Psychology exam content, appearing in both multiple-choice questions and free-response sections. Questions typically ask you to:

  • Identify brain structures and their functions
  • Explain how neurons communicate
  • Describe how genetics influence behavior
  • Connect biological processes to psychological phenomena
  • Interpret research findings related to biological psychology

Study Tips for Unit 1

  • Use visual aids

    Create or study diagrams of brain regions, neurons, and neural communication. Visual representation helps with remembering structures and their relationships.

  • Connect structure to function

    Instead of memorizing lists, focus on understanding how the structure of each component relates to its function.

  • Make real-world connections

    Relate biological concepts to everyday experiences (e.g., how caffeine affects neurotransmitters, why we feel jet lag).

  • Study one system at a time

    Master each component of the nervous system before moving on to the next. Build your knowledge systematically.

1.1 Interaction of Heredity and Environment

The nature vs. nurture debate explores how genetics (nature) and environment (nurture) influence psychological traits and behaviors. Modern psychology recognizes that both factors interact in complex ways to shape who we are.

Key Concept: Gene-Environment Interaction

Genetic predispositions and environmental factors work together to influence behavior and development. Genes can affect how sensitive we are to environmental factors, while environments can determine whether certain genetic tendencies are expressed.

Research Methods in Behavioral Genetics

Twin Studies

Comparing identical twins (who share 100% of their genes) with fraternal twins (who share about 50%) helps researchers estimate the genetic contribution to traits. Higher similarity in identical twins suggests stronger genetic influence.

Example: Studies show that identical twins have more similar IQ scores (correlation of about 0.85) than fraternal twins (correlation of about 0.6), suggesting a significant genetic component to intelligence.

Adoption Studies

Examining individuals raised by non-biological parents helps separate genetic and environmental influences. Similarities to biological parents suggest genetic influence, while similarities to adoptive parents indicate environmental influence.

Example: Adopted children's intelligence correlates more with their biological parents than their adoptive parents, suggesting significant genetic influence on intelligence.

Family Studies

Examining trait prevalence among biological relatives of different degrees helps determine heritability. The more closely related individuals are, the more similar they should be in heritable traits.

Example: The risk of developing schizophrenia increases with genetic relatedness to someone with the disorder, from about 1% in the general population to 48% for the identical twin of someone with schizophrenia.

Important Terminology

Heritability

The proportion of variance in a trait within a population that can be attributed to genetic factors. Heritability estimates range from 0 to 1, with higher numbers indicating stronger genetic influence.

Epigenetics

The study of how environmental factors can affect gene expression without changing the DNA sequence. Environmental factors can turn genes "on" or "off" through chemical modifications.

Range of Reaction

The concept that genes establish a range of possible outcomes, while environmental influences determine where within that range a person falls.

Phenotype & Genotype

Genotype is the genetic makeup, while phenotype is the observable characteristics resulting from interaction of genotype with the environment.

Concept Flashcard

What is heritability and how is it misunderstood?

Heritability measures how much variation in a trait within a population is due to genetic variation.

Common misconceptions:

  • Heritability does NOT indicate how much of an individual's trait is genetic
  • High heritability does NOT mean a trait cannot be changed by environment
  • Heritability estimates apply to populations, not individuals

Real-World Application: Understanding Gene-Environment Interaction

Consider the relationship between genetics and alcoholism:

  • Some people have genetic variations that affect how alcohol is metabolized or how pleasurable it feels.
  • These genetic factors can increase susceptibility to alcohol dependence.
  • However, environmental factors like family attitudes toward drinking, peer pressure, and stress levels also play crucial roles.
  • A person with genetic risk factors may never develop alcoholism if raised in an environment where alcohol is not available or drinking is discouraged.
  • Conversely, environmental pressure might lead to alcohol problems even in those without genetic predispositions.

This illustrates how genes and environment work together, rather than independently, to influence behavior and psychological outcomes.

1.2 Overview of the Nervous System

The nervous system is the body's communication network, consisting of billions of neurons that transmit information throughout the body. It's organized into two main divisions: the central nervous system and the peripheral nervous system.

Structure of the Nervous System

Central Nervous System (CNS)

Consists of the brain and spinal cord. The CNS interprets incoming information and formulates responses.

  • Protected by skull and vertebrae
  • Further protected by meninges (protective membranes)
  • Surrounded by cerebrospinal fluid
  • Protected by blood-brain barrier
Peripheral Nervous System (PNS)

Consists of all neural tissue outside the CNS. The PNS connects the CNS to the rest of the body.

  • Includes all nerves throughout the body
  • Transmits sensory information to the CNS
  • Carries motor commands from the CNS
  • Divided into somatic and autonomic systems

Divisions of the Peripheral Nervous System

Somatic Nervous System

Controls voluntary movements and transmits sensory information from the skin, muscles, and joints.

  • Voluntary control
  • Involved in skeletal muscle movement
  • Sensory information from external environment
Autonomic Nervous System

Regulates internal organs and glands, typically operating below the level of consciousness.

  • Involuntary control
  • Maintains homeostasis
  • Regulates heart rate, digestion, respiration, etc.

The Role of the Endocrine System

While not part of the nervous system, the endocrine system works closely with it to regulate bodily functions. The endocrine system uses hormones released into the bloodstream to influence target organs, while the nervous system uses electrical impulses for more immediate effects.

The hypothalamus serves as a key connection point between these systems, regulating the pituitary gland which controls many other endocrine glands.

Concept Flashcard

What are the key differences between the sympathetic and parasympathetic divisions?

Sympathetic Division ("Fight-or-Flight")

  • Activates during stress or emergency
  • Increases heart rate and blood pressure
  • Dilates pupils and airways
  • Decreases digestive activity

Parasympathetic Division ("Rest-and-Digest")

  • Dominates during relaxed states
  • Decreases heart rate and blood pressure
  • Constricts pupils
  • Increases digestive activity

Real-World Application: Stress Response

Understanding the nervous system helps explain how the body responds to stress:

Short-term Stress Response
  1. The brain (part of CNS) perceives a threat
  2. Signals sent to the hypothalamus
  3. Sympathetic division activates
  4. Adrenal glands release adrenaline
  5. Body experiences "fight-or-flight" response: increased heart rate, rapid breathing, heightened alertness
  6. After threat passes, parasympathetic division restores calm
Chronic Stress Consequences

Prolonged activation of the sympathetic system due to chronic stress can lead to:

  • Weakened immune system
  • Digestive problems
  • Sleep disturbances
  • Cardiovascular issues
  • Memory and concentration problems

This example shows how multiple divisions of the nervous system work together, along with the endocrine system, to respond to environmental challenges.

1.3 The Neuron and Neural Firing

Neurons are the fundamental units of the nervous system, specialized cells designed to transmit information through electrical and chemical signals. The human brain contains approximately 86 billion neurons, forming an intricate network that enables all mental processes.

Neuron Structure

Neurons have a distinctive structure specialized for communication:

Cell Body (Soma)

Contains the nucleus and maintains the cell's life processes. Integrates incoming information from dendrites.

Dendrites

Branch-like extensions that receive signals from other neurons. They increase the surface area for receiving input.

Axon

Long fiber that conducts electrical impulses away from the cell body toward other neurons, muscles, or glands.

Myelin Sheath

Fatty insulation that covers some axons. Increases the speed of neural transmission through saltatory conduction.

Nodes of Ranvier

Gaps in the myelin sheath where the action potential "jumps" during saltatory conduction.

Terminal Buttons

Bulb-like endings of the axon branches that contain neurotransmitters. Release chemicals into the synapse.

Types of Neurons

Sensory Neurons

Carry information from sensory receptors to the central nervous system. Example: neurons that transmit pain signals from your finger when you touch something hot.

Motor Neurons

Carry commands from the central nervous system to muscles and glands. Example: neurons that signal your leg muscles to contract when walking.

Interneurons

Connect sensory and motor neurons within the central nervous system. Example: neurons in the spinal cord that process and relay signals between incoming sensory and outgoing motor neurons.

The Action Potential

Neurons communicate through electrical signals called action potentials. This process involves changes in the electrical charge across the neuron's membrane:

1
Resting Potential

When inactive, the neuron maintains a negative charge (about -70mV) inside relative to outside, due to differences in ion concentrations.

2
Depolarization

When the neuron receives sufficient stimulation, sodium channels open, allowing positive sodium ions to rush in. This makes the inside of the cell more positive.

3
Threshold

If the membrane potential reaches the threshold level (about -55mV), an action potential is triggered. This is an "all-or-nothing" response.

4
Action Potential

The membrane potential rapidly reverses, becoming positive inside. This spike of electricity travels down the axon.

5
Repolarization

Potassium channels open, allowing positive potassium ions to flow out. The membrane potential returns to negative.

6
Refractory Period

A brief period during which the neuron cannot fire again, as ion concentrations return to normal.

Synaptic Transmission

Neurons communicate with other neurons across synapses, the tiny gaps between cells:

1
Action Potential Arrival

The action potential travels down the axon and reaches the terminal buttons.

2
Neurotransmitter Release

Calcium ions enter the terminal, triggering synaptic vesicles to fuse with the cell membrane and release neurotransmitters into the synaptic cleft.

3
Receptor Binding

Neurotransmitters cross the synaptic cleft and bind to specific receptor sites on the receiving neuron's dendrites or cell body.

4
Postsynaptic Potential

Binding causes ion channels to open or close, creating either excitatory postsynaptic potentials (EPSPs) that make the neuron more likely to fire, or inhibitory postsynaptic potentials (IPSPs) that make it less likely to fire.

5
Neurotransmitter Inactivation

Neurotransmitters are quickly removed from the synaptic cleft through reuptake, enzymatic breakdown, or diffusion to terminate the signal.

Neural Firing Flashcard

What is the "all-or-nothing" principle in neural firing?

The "all-or-nothing" principle refers to the fact that an action potential either happens completely or not at all.

Key points:

  • If stimulation is below threshold, no action potential occurs
  • Once threshold is reached, the action potential proceeds at full strength
  • Action potentials are uniform in size and strength
  • Neurons communicate intensity through firing frequency, not action potential size

Real-World Application: How Drugs Affect Neural Communication

Understanding neural transmission helps explain how various psychoactive substances affect the brain:

Stimulants (e.g., Cocaine, Amphetamines)

Increase activity of dopamine and norepinephrine by:

  • Blocking reuptake (cocaine)
  • Stimulating release (amphetamines)
  • Results: increased alertness, euphoria, reduced appetite
Depressants (e.g., Alcohol, Benzodiazepines)

Enhance effects of GABA, the main inhibitory neurotransmitter:

  • GABA opens chloride ion channels
  • Inhibits neural firing
  • Results: sedation, reduced anxiety, impaired coordination
Antidepressants (e.g., SSRIs)

Affect serotonin levels in synapses:

  • Block serotonin reuptake from synaptic cleft
  • Increase available serotonin
  • Results: mood elevation (after sustained use)
Opioids (e.g., Morphine, Heroin)

Mimic endorphins, our natural painkillers:

  • Bind to opioid receptors
  • Inhibit pain signals
  • Trigger dopamine release
  • Results: pain relief, euphoria, respiratory depression
Hallucinogens (e.g., LSD)

Primarily affect serotonin systems:

  • Bind to specific serotonin receptors
  • Alter sensory processing and mood
  • Results: perceptual distortions, altered consciousness
Tolerance and Dependence

Repeated exposure leads to neuroadaptations:

  • Down-regulation of receptors
  • Changes in neurotransmitter production
  • Result: need for more drug to achieve same effect
  • Withdrawal when drug is removed

This application shows how understanding the mechanisms of neural communication is essential for explaining drug effects, addiction, and treatment approaches.

1.4 The Brain

The human brain is the center of the nervous system, containing approximately 86 billion neurons. It's responsible for all thought, sensation, emotion, and behavior. The brain can be divided into several major regions, each with specialized functions.

Major Brain Structures

The Brainstem

The most primitive part of the brain, connecting the spinal cord to the rest of the brain. It controls basic life functions.

Medulla

Controls vital autonomic functions including heartbeat, breathing, blood pressure, and swallowing.

Pons

Helps coordinate movement and plays a role in sleep, arousal, and breathing regulation.

Reticular Formation

A network of nuclei critical for regulating arousal, attention, and sleep-wake transitions.

The Cerebellum

Located at the back of the brain, beneath the cerebral hemispheres. Though only 10% of the brain's volume, it contains over 50% of all neurons.

  • Coordinates voluntary movement and balance
  • Helps with motor learning and muscle memory
  • Plays a role in certain cognitive functions
  • Damage results in jerky, uncoordinated movements
The Limbic System

A collection of structures involved in emotion, motivation, memory, and learning.

Amygdala

Processes emotions, especially fear and aggression. Critical for emotional learning and memory.

Hippocampus

Essential for forming new long-term memories. Damage results in anterograde amnesia.

Hypothalamus

Regulates homeostasis (body temperature, hunger, thirst, sleep). Links nervous and endocrine systems.

Thalamus

Major sensory relay center that filters information going to the cortex. The "gateway" to consciousness.

The Cerebral Cortex

The outermost layer of the cerebrum, composed of gray matter (neuron cell bodies). It's wrinkled to increase surface area and is divided into two hemispheres.

Frontal Lobe

Involved in higher cognitive functions, decision making, planning, personality, and motor control. Contains the prefrontal cortex, critical for executive function.

Parietal Lobe

Processes somatosensory information (touch, temperature, pain). Important for spatial awareness and attention.

Temporal Lobe

Processes auditory information and involved in language comprehension. Contains structures important for memory.

Occipital Lobe

Processes visual information. Different areas handle different aspects of vision (color, motion, form).

Specialized Areas within the Cortex

Primary Sensory Areas
  • Primary Visual Cortex: Processes basic visual information like edges, orientation
  • Primary Auditory Cortex: Processes basic sound information like pitch, volume
  • Primary Somatosensory Cortex: Processes touch, temperature, and pain from body parts
  • Gustatory Cortex: Processes taste
  • Olfactory Cortex: Processes smell
Association Areas
  • Wernicke's Area: Language comprehension (temporal lobe)
  • Broca's Area: Speech production (frontal lobe)
  • Prefrontal Cortex: Executive functions, personality, decision-making
  • Angular Gyrus: Cross-modal associations, reading
  • Motor Association Cortex: Movement planning and coordination

Cerebral Lateralization and Hemispheric Specialization

Left vs. Right Hemisphere

While both hemispheres work together, they show some specialization:

Left Hemisphere

Typically dominant for:

  • Language processing (in most people)
  • Logical and analytical thinking
  • Sequential processing
  • Mathematical calculations
Right Hemisphere

Typically dominant for:

  • Spatial abilities and face recognition
